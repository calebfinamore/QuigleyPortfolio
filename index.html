Course Goals


1. Consider technology’s role in acts of making

Technology allows us to do things we could have never done before. Zooming in on an album cover to examine specific details, creating web pages to introduce ourselves, and having artificial intelligence try to come up with a logo are things that are only possible because of technology. However, it's also important to note that some of these services come at a premium price point, which can make the act of creating feel inaccessible to those in less fortunate financial circumstances.

2. Leverage Design Principles.

In the first few weeks of class, we learned about essential design principles such as alignment, repititon, proximity, and contrast. We also learned more specific techniques, such as how to read HTML, how to use InDesign, and how to test our designs with users. While each of these projects incorporated different principles, they all ultimately focused on carrying the spirit of good design that marries form and function.

3.Recognize and respond to ethical concerns.

As I mentioned previously, I would be remiss if when discussing the advantages of technology within creative spaces, I did not also point out the financial barrier that stands between many people and the ability to design in the modern fashion. While we are lucky to have a number of open source tools that can perform many of the same functions, the fact of the matter is that Adobe and their Creative Suite are industry standard, despite business practices that are at best, anti-consumer and at worst, predatory. The University of Pittsburgh provides its students with access to Adobe Creative Suite and all its products, but I would encourage anybody without that complimentary access to explore open-source alternatives (or piracy).

4. Engage with others in a workshop environment

So far I’ve been just getting to know some of my classmates, but I’ve really enjoyed the time I’ve spent with them. They’ve all been extremely supportive of both mine and others’ work, but also unafraid to offer suggestions and advice when appropriate. It’s been awesome to have people to bounce ideas off of, ask for help, and just generally interact with in a small group setting.

5. Practice revision within and across media forms

While I have a decent amount of design experience, I definitely have the least practice in InDesign. This class so far has given me a great opportunity to improve my skills in that app, and along with that has come a lot of revising. In particular, I spent a lot of time making small tweaks and improvements to my Good Design project. Playing with the layout of the page and tying the descriptive text to the zoomed details took a lot of incremental adjustments, and even now I’m not totally satisfied with the result. Regardless, I’m appreciating the time I’m spending improving both my skills and my work through revision.

6. Learn to present your work

I actually have public speaking experience dating back to my Catholic middle school. Our priest held a mandatory public speaking class for eighth graders where he taught the importance of being able to present ideas clearly and fluidly. It’s been a while since then, but I consider that my foundation of oratory skill. I haven’t really given any presentations in a while due to COVID, so I’ve enjoyed the low-stakes stages for working back into public speaking and presentation this class has provided. In particular, the UX Design project was a new experience that I really enjoyed speaking about and discussing with my audience of peers.
